### The art (and science) of cost estimation and Earned Value Management for project management
Most projects are delivered over budget or behind schedule. In my
expereince, people are bad at estimating how long things will take or
how much they will cost. But we can use tools to help us accurately
estimate project costs and track progress against those estimates.
Whether you're building a skyscraper, launching a new software
application, or organizing a major event, getting the numbers right can
make or break your initiative.

Cost estimation and earned value management can be tricky. From the
natural human tendency toward optimism to the unpredictable nature of
real-world projects, there are many ways these efforts can go awry.
Fortunately, by understanding the key principles and best practices, you
can navigate these complexities and leverage data-driven insights to
keep your projects on track.

#### Why estimation is hard
At the heart of cost estimation challenges lies a fundamental human
bias --- the planning fallacy. Put simply, we have a natural inclination
to underestimate how long things will take and how much they will cost.
This optimism stems from several psychological factors:

1.  [Lack of Historical Data: When we're embarking on a new initiative,
    we often lack a robust dataset of past performance to draw from.
    Without that empirical foundation, we're left to rely on gut
    feelings, best guesses, and incomplete information. This leads to
    unrealistic timelines and budgets.]
2.  [Overconfidence: Even when we do have some historical context, we
    tend to discount the likelihood of future uncertainties and
    roadblocks. We convince ourselves that "this time will be
    different" --- that we'll avoid the pitfalls that plagued previous
    efforts. This overconfidence further skews our estimates.]
3.  [Scope Creep: As projects progress, the natural tendency is for the
    scope to expand, often incrementally and organically. Features get
    added, requirements evolve, and before you know it, the undertaking
    has grown far beyond its original parameters. Accounting for this
    moving target is notoriously difficult.]
4.  [Misaligned Incentives: In some cases, cost, and schedule estimates
    are intentionally manipulated, either consciously or subconsciously.
    Project team members may have an incentive to lowball timelines and
    budgets to win bids or appear more efficient. Executives, meanwhile,
    may pressure for unrealistic targets to meet business
    objectives.]

The result of these factors is that project after project falls victim
to delays, cost overruns, and unmet expectations. Studies show that the
more than 90% of initiatives fail to meet their original budget and
timeline commitments.

#### Earned Value Management
But, project managers have a useful tool at their disposal: earned value
management (EVM). This data-driven approach allows you to accurately
track progress against your cost and schedule baselines, providing an
objective assessment of project health.

The core concept of EVM is straightforward: you establish planned values
for the work that should be completed by any given point in time, and
then you measure the value of the work that has actually been
accomplished. This gives you three key metrics:

1\. Planned Value (PV): The budgeted cost of work scheduled to be
completed by a certain date. ([Gantt
charts](https://medium.com/@kylejones_47003/the-benefits-of-using-gantt-charts-in-project-management-dc5990d504f6) can be useful here to visualize this)\
2. Earned Value (EV): The value of work actually completed by that
date.\
3. Actual Cost (AC): The total costs incurred in executing the work to
date.

From these three data points, you can calculate a number of useful
ratios and variances:

• Cost Variance (CV) = EV --- AC\
 --- Indicates whether you're over or under budget

• Schedule Variance (SV) = EV --- PV \
 --- Reflects whether you're ahead of or behind schedule

• Cost Performance Index (CPI) = EV / AC\
 --- Measures the efficiency of your cost spending

• Schedule Performance Index (SPI) = EV / PV\
 --- Gauges the efficiency of your schedule execution

These metrics give you an at-a-glance view of your project's
performance, allowing you to spot emerging issues and take corrective
action before they spiral out of control. Rather than relying on gut
feelings or informal status updates, you have a reliable, data-driven
dashboard.

Putting EVM into Practice\
The true power of earned value management comes in how you apply it.
Here are some best practices for leveraging EVM to improve your project
outcomes:

1.  [Establish Comprehensive Baselines: The foundation of EVM is your
    initial cost and schedule baselines. Take the time upfront to
    thoughtfully define the scope of work, estimate resource needs, and
    build a realistic timeline. The more rigorous and well-documented
    these baselines are, the more value you'll get from the EVM
    process.]
2.  [Measure Progress Frequently: Don't wait until the end of a project
    phase or milestone to take your EVM measurements. Capture
    performance data on a regular cadence --- weekly or monthly, at a
    minimum. This allows you to course-correct quickly based on trends,
    rather than waiting for major problems to arise.]
3.  [Tie EVM to Project Processes: Integrate EVM seamlessly into your
    broader project management workflows. Ensure all team members
    understand how to track and report on the required metrics.
    Incorporate EVM reviews into your regular status meetings and
    governance processes.]
4.  [Analyze Variances Rigorously: When you identify cost or schedule
    variances, don't just note the numbers. Dig into the root
    causes --- what's driving the over/under performance? Understand the
    implications and develop concrete action plans to address
    unfavorable trends.]
5.  [Communicate Proactively: One of the biggest benefits of EVM is its
    ability to provide early warning of potential issues. Leverage these
    insights to have transparent, data-driven conversations with
    stakeholders. Don't wait until problems escalate to share bad
    news --- get in front of it.]
6.  [Continuously Improve: Track your EVM metrics over time, looking for
    patterns and opportunities to refine your estimation and tracking
    processes. What's working well, and where are you still missing the
    mark? Capture lessons learned to inform future projects.]

#### Overcoming the Limitations of EVM
Earned value management is useful but it's not a silver bullet. There
are a few key limitations to be aware of:

- Subjectivity in Earned Value Calculations: Determining the "value" of
  work completed can involve a fair bit of judgment, especially for
  complex, multifaceted projects. Defining and consistently measuring
  this metric isn't always straightforward.
- Difficulty Estimating at Project Outset: Accurate initial cost and
  schedule baselines are critical for EVM. But nailing down these
  estimates, particularly for novel initiatives, can be extremely
  challenging.
- Potential for Manipulation: Just as with any performance metric,
  there's a risk that EVM data could be intentionally skewed or gamed to
  present a rosier picture. Vigilance is required to maintain
  integrity.

#### Limitations in Handling Scope Changes
While EVM can accommodate some scope adjustments, major changes to the
project baseline can undermine the value of historical EVM data and
trend analysis.

The key is to recognize these limitations and address them proactively.
Invest time in defining clear, objective EVM processes. Build in
multiple layers of validation. And remain flexible enough to adapt your
approach as project circumstances evolve.

#### Pairing EVM with Other Estimation Techniques
To further strengthen your cost estimation capabilities, consider
complementing earned value management with other quantitative and
qualitative methods:

- Analogous Estimating: Look to the outcomes of similar past projects
  to inform your current estimates. What did it cost and how long did it
  take to deliver comparable work?
- Parametric Modeling: Use statistical analysis to develop predictive
  models based on historical data and key project variables. This can
  uncover meaningful relationships and patterns.
- Expert Judgment: Leverage the experience and domain knowledge of
  subject matter experts, whether internal or external. Their insights
  can provide a valuable reality check.
- Three-Point Estimating (PERT): Calculate a weighted average duration
  or cost by considering optimistic, pessimistic, and most-likely
  scenarios. This accounts for uncertainty.

#### Agile Estimation Techniques
In iterative, incremental delivery models, techniques like story points
and velocity tracking can supplement traditional EVM.

By blending these diverse approaches, you gain a richer, more holistic
understanding of project feasibility and risk.

#### Overcoming the Cost Estimation Challenge
Mastering cost estimation and earned value management is not easy. It
requires diligence, objectivity, and a willingness to confront harsh
realities head-on. But the payoff is immense --- the ability to keep
your projects on track, satisfy stakeholders, and achieve meaningful
results.

Start by tackling the natural human biases that skew our forecasts.
Cultivate a culture of data-driven decision making, where team members
feel empowered and expected to challenge unrealistic assumptions. Build
a comprehensive, transparent EVM system that provides early warning of
emerging issues.

Remain vigilant and adaptable. Project dynamics are always in flux, so
your estimation and tracking processes must evolve accordingly. Leverage
lessons learned to continuously refine your approach.

Cost estimation and earned value management may not be the most
glamorous aspects of project leadership. But master them, and you'll
unlock the keys to consistent, predictable project success.
